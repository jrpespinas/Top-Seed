## Overall Impression

The design system under this feature is production-quality. The stacked side A/vs/B layout is a genuine insight. But the interaction model for the single most important action â€” assigning a matchup to a court â€” has a fundamental spatial problem: the court picker detaches from the card and appears in a different part of the screen. That one issue erodes the operational confidence the organizer needs to trust this tool. Everything else on the list feeds from or around that central failure.

## What's Working

**1. Stacked A/vs/B layout.** Left/right column layouts for doubles matchups force the organizer to mentally map "top-left = partner." The vertical stack with a "vs" divider reads in natural scan order â€” my team, then their team â€” matching how a coach would write it on a whiteboard. This is the right call and a real UX improvement.

**2. Design token system.** Raw OKLCH channel values, semantic aliases, correctly recalculated light/dark tokens (not just inverted), `MotionConfig reducedMotion="user"` at every motion boundary, and the Web Animations API pulse checked against `prefers-reduced-motion` before firing. This is disciplined, not incidental.

**3. Confirm-before-remove in the queue.** The AnimatePresence cross-fade between the action row and the confirm row is non-intrusive and correct for a courtside tool. Accidental removal with no undo would be a session-disrupting incident. The implementation (lines 128â€“190 in PlayerPoolColumn.tsx) handles the confirm/cancel state cleanly.

## Priority Issues

### [P0] Court picker has no spatial relationship to the card it acts on

**Why it matters**: This affects every single assignment â€” the highest-frequency action in the matchup surface. In `MatchupColumn`, the court picker animates in at the bottom of the column, below all the other cards. With 3â€“4 planning cards, the triggering card is off-screen when the picker appears. The user loses the spatial link between "which matchup" and "which court." Under courtside time pressure, this causes wrong-court assignments with no recovery confirmation.

**Fix**: Move the court picker inside `PlanningCard` â€” render it as an inline expansion within the card footer, replacing the Assign button row with a list of available courts when in "picking" state. The card and the court selection stay in one visual unit. This requires passing court data and the pick handler into `PlanningCard` as props, but eliminates the spatial problem entirely.

**Suggested command**: `/impeccable polish PlanningCard`

---

### [P1] The Assign CTA is undersized for its operational weight

**Why it matters**: `text-xs font-semibold px-3 py-1.5 min-h-[36px]` â€” 36px height on the primary action of a courtside tool, left-aligned with content sizing. The PRODUCT.md spec calls for 44px minimum on interactive elements and 48px on primary session actions (Start Match, Assign). This button hits neither. The copper fill is correct but the footprint is too small for a standing, one-handed, glancing interaction.

**Fix**: `w-full py-2.5` (achieves ~44px). Remove the "Ready" label to the right â€” the button's copper state already signals readiness. The freed horizontal space and vertical padding give the CTA the weight it needs.

**Suggested command**: `/impeccable polish PlanningCard`

---

### [P1] QueueMatchupSection and MatchupColumn have diverged

**Why it matters**: One of these components is the authoritative matchup surface. They already differ: `QueueMatchupSection` doesn't pass `onPlayerDrop` or `onRemovePlayer` to `PlanningCard` (so drag-to-fill and chip removal are silently absent), court labels show bare numbers instead of "Court N", and the Add button shows only an icon (no text label). Whichever layout ships, the behavior contract should be identical.

**Fix**: Either (a) consolidate into one canonical component, or (b) add a `readOnly` prop to `PlanningCard` that explicitly suppresses drag/remove affordances when the surface doesn't support them, and fix the court label in `QueueMatchupSection` line 278: `Court {c.number}`.

**Suggested command**: `/impeccable harden QueueMatchupSection`

---

### [P2] Disabled RefreshCw button should not ship

**Why it matters**: `disabled opacity-40 cursor-not-allowed title="Smart suggest coming soon"` is placeholder chrome. It trains users to tap a broken control, takes space in a tight 252px card header, and the `title` tooltip is not accessible on touch. After a few sessions of tapping it, experienced organizers distrust the interface's completeness.

**Fix**: Remove it until the smart suggest feature is built. The card header becomes: match type toggle | grip | dismiss. Three elements, all functional.

**Suggested command**: `/impeccable distill PlanningCard`

---

### [P2] Chip-swap mode has no cancel affordance

**Why it matters**: Selecting a chip enters swap mode. The only escape is tapping the same chip again â€” not obvious, and not visible. The swap hint says "Tap another player to swap" but shows no cancel. On mobile with fat-finger taps, accidental chip selection is frequent. A user who selects the wrong chip has no clearly visible way out.

**Fix**: Add a cancel button next to the swap hint: `<button onClick={() => setSelectedChip(null)} className="text-[10px] text-muted hover:text-ink">Cancel</button>`. Two lines.

**Suggested command**: `/impeccable harden PlanningCard`

- The assignment pulse animation fires when state reaches `"ready"` (line 109â€“124), not when the organizer confirms the assignment. The peak emotional signal is being delivered at the wrong moment â€” at proposal-ready, not at dispatch.

## Questions to Consider

- **The picker detachment is the core problem: should the court picker live inside the card or stay external?** Moving it inside the card (inline expansion) keeps the assignment decision spatial. Keeping it external is simpler to implement but breaks the courtside mental model. The inline approach requires accepting court data as a card prop â€” is that a prop-drilling problem given the current architecture, or manageable?
- **Which layout is canonical â€” QueueMatchupSection or MatchupColumn?** Two diverged implementations of the same feature is a maintenance liability. The answer should drive which one gets hardened and which becomes a wrapper or alias.
- **Is there a success state needed after assignment, or is the organizer trusted to know it worked?** The PRODUCT.md says "operational confidence over delight" and "predictability and speed beat wow." A silent card disappearance might be the right call for speed. But if assignment can silently fail, the organizer needs a signal. The answer depends on whether assignment is currently synchronous/infallible in the mock or will involve async server calls.
